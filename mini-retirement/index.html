My name is Caleb. I’m 24 years old, and I’m retiring.

I know, it’s a bait­ing title, but I really am retir­ing. A mini-retirement. Lis­ten up.

19 months ago I moved into my cur­rent apart­ment. Two months into the full-time Amer­i­can work­force, I took some new inter­ests. The stock mar­ket fas­ci­nated me. My rapidly expand­ing list of RSS feeds began includ­ing more per­sonal finance and fru­gal­ity blogs. Given the hous­ing bub­ble, hav­ing just moved into an apart­ment, and liv­ing in one of the most expen­sive small towns in the nation, real estate took a spe­cial place in the front of my mind.

Inter­ested in houses and want to lose your­self for hours, days, weeks? Browse inter­na­tional real estate list­ings. I would exer­cise extreme cau­tion before actu­ally going down an actual pur­chase path, but look around. It’s fuck­ing house porn. The idea of for­eign real estate was a dreamy escape, and while I oohed and aahed at the cas­tles that could be pur­chased for the price of a San Luis Obispo town­house, I main­tained a sense of detach­ment. My brain didn’t label it a real pos­si­bil­ity until I ran across one listing.

Two bed­room house on a golf course in cen­tral Thai­land. $89,000 USD.

The crazy thoughts that crossed my mind at that point would even­tu­ally evolve into the sit­u­a­tion I’ve place myself in today.

I’m mov­ing to China.

On Day 100 I’ll leave San Luis Obispo, drive north to Corn­ing, and stay with the folks for 10 days. After that?


View Escape Route in a larger map

SXSW brings me to Austin. I’ll be vis­it­ing my sis­ter in Kailua. Van­cou­ver? Well, it was the cheap­est way to Hong Kong by hun­dreds of dol­lars. And I get to spend an unex­pected day in Vancouver!

What the sched­ule doesn’t show is a sub­way ride to Shen­zhen, a flight to Zhengzhou to meet up with E, and a train back to Shen­zhen weeks after that. There I’ll set­tle in.

It takes more than a short jump to go from read­ing a Thai real estate list­ing online to relo­cat­ing to China. There are no sure­fire steps to get from one to the other. I can only tell you my own story.

Acquire knowl­edge.

I read per­sonal finance and fru­gal­ity blogs. Don’t know what I’m talk­ing about? Try these:

  • Get Rich Slowly
  • I Will Teach You To Be Rich
  • The Sim­ple Dollar

The over­rid­ing theme of these blogs is pretty sim­ple. Don’t spend money on things you don’t care about. Track your finance and main­tain a bud­get. Find ways to make more money. If you’ve lived a life of uncon­trol­lable debt and uncon­scious spend­ing, you’ll find a good deal of refresh­ing advice.

I opened up an ING sub­ac­count. I named it “Thai­land.” I didn’t really plan on sav­ing for that exact house, but I knew what it rep­re­sented. I set an auto­matic trans­fer from my main check­ing account where my work income is direct deposited to the ING sub­ac­count. This hands-off automa­tion sys­tem allows me to save for large pur­chases that I can fore­see months ahead. It would take for­ever, but $350 made the silent move each month. With things on autopi­lot I sat back, waited, and con­tin­ued reading.

Very few books, even incred­i­ble ones that you’ll remem­ber for­ever, will lit­er­ally change your life. Vagabond­ing changed mine. I had read oth­ers, includ­ing The 4-Hour Work Week, which one could argue pro­vides the blue­print for this whole mini-retirement of mine. Shit, it is Tim Fer­riss’ term. But Vagabond­ing pro­vided crys­tal­ized details for all the thoughts that had been float­ing aim­lessly around my head. It showed me that this is some­thing all peo­ple can do, some peo­ple actu­ally do, and I should do.

Just read­ing a book never changes your life; doing some­thing about the read­ing is required. It took me three months to digest what I’d read, gather some sack, and make the first big deci­sion. In the final days of 2008 I took aim, set a rea­son­able goal, and laid out a plan. Save $10,000. Fly to Thai­land. Live for 8–14 months.

Take action, one day at a time.

The keys to arriv­ing safely at a long term goal are chunk­ing and automa­tion. Take a giant goal and break it down into com­pos­ite steps. Take those steps and break them down into even smaller steps. Rinse and repeat until the steps are easy enough to accom­plish in one day. It’s a very Get­ting Things Done approach to life goals.

Let’s work this out back­wards. I want to live 8–14 months in Thai­land. What’s stand­ing in my way? Believe me, there are a mul­ti­tude of answers to this, and I may some­day address why some of them are valid or not, but the biggest obsta­cle is almost always money. How much money will I need? Let’s say $10,000. What is that spread over a year? 18 months? Two years? Start ask­ing your­self, “Can I set that aside?!” This is where a fru­gal lifestyle and side income really pay off. Through tight liv­ing, free­lance work, and an auto­mated sav­ing sched­ule, I was able to amass the nec­es­sary green in 11 months, beat­ing my 12 month goal.

With finances auto­mated, I was free to tackle other obsta­cles. This web­site is my way of address­ing a major one: stuff. I knew I didn’t want to pay $50 a month just to have my shit go unused in a shed some­where for a year. “Get rid of your stuff” is hardly a goal that can be accom­plished in one day, so I chun­ked it into 100. For all the travel web­sites I visit, I feel like the details of this step to a life of long term travel is some­times glanced over. You’ll often see, “Put up your stuff for sale and make some extra money.” As some peo­ple are find­ing out, sell­ing things, espe­cially now, is a dif­fi­cult feat. While tech­ni­cally cor­rect, “sell all your extra stuff” is not action­able advice. This should at least be sum­ma­rized, “Sell your extra stuff. It will not be easy. If you can’t sell it, throw it away any­way. And start early.”

There is another major obsta­cle keep­ing most of us from board­ing a plane to the other side of the world. Work. Sav­ing money for the trip frees us from the fis­cal neces­sity of work, but does lit­tle to quiet the part of our brain that makes us feel bad about quit­ting. There is a guilt that accom­pa­nies for­sak­ing a job and schlep­ping around the globe with­out a care, while every­one you know back home is liv­ing in Out­look and Excel. In this regard, hav­ing a side job that you can turn and focus on and try to grow is an immea­sur­able ben­e­fit. If you don’t have this lux­ury, but have always thought about start­ing a side busi­ness, this is as good an oppor­tu­nity as ever to start. Some peo­ple con­vince their cur­rent jobs to let them relo­cate and con­tinue work­ing remotely, and that’s great too if you love what you do. But if none of this sounds like you yet, I’m not going to try to offer any fur­ther advice on quit­ting. It’s some tough shit, and you’ll have to find a way to be com­fort­able with it.

Remov­ing this obsta­cle is fresh in my mind. I wrote my res­ig­na­tion email today.

Jump off a bridge.

There are lit­tle things — secur­ing a visa, pass­port, travel insur­ance, nec­es­sary shots — that must be addressed, but there is one last major step, which I’ll just call Doing The Damn Thing. It can be bro­ken down into three actions, all doable in a day, some­times minutes.

  1. Pick a date. Make it real. Cir­cle a day on your cal­en­dar. Don’t make plans with peo­ple for any­thing past the date. Keep your sched­ule flex­i­ble for the days lead­ing up to the date, to han­dle any unfore­seen prob­lems that come up. Tell any­thing with ears what your date is to the point where peo­ple wish that you would shut the fuck up.
  2. Buy a ticket. Drop­ping hun­dreds or more on non-refundable air­faire has the added effect of mak­ing you feel invested and com­mit­ted to going.
  3. Get on the damn plane. I can’t speak much about this, since I haven’t done it yet!

Here is where my story ends, for now. Every­thing is in place and all I need to do is step out of the way, let things hap­pen, not med­dle and fuck things up, and when the time comes, do the damn thing.
